9treatment-regulation
There are many elements of 'wellness management'. Treatment means pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy. Medication is a foundation, psychotherapy reduces relapse, non-compliance, and develops the skills to regulate. Medication typically means mood stabalisers, anti-psychotics, anti-depressants, anxiolytics, sleep medication, and so on. Mood stabalisers are used to try and anchor state and will generally be core to treatment. There a few different types, such as lithium, and people react very differently to them. Titration onto these medications is generally a slow process, as side effects have to be monitored and people react differently. Certain types of anti-depressants might be used to soften the lower states, these also have to be carefully monitored, and in fact many well-known anti-depressants used for treating unipolar depression are known to worsen symptoms of bipolar disorder. For the states at the higher end of the spectrum, sedatives can be used, hypnotics for sleep, and anti-psychotics. A medical regime often takes a long time to establish, and needs monitoring and adjustment, and there will be compromises - some medications will reduce the risk of mania, but increase sedation for example. However, over time many people will become good at monitoring their state and be able to adjust medication.
10support
Support means the people around you - people you trust who know the condition and know you, who can support and help identify when you are nearing danger zones. They are generally given your wellness plan, so they know what to advise: increase medication, reduce stress. With some, you may agree in advance that they can intervene - for example, if you are not adhering to agreed steps like medication, you allow them to take control. This is hard. It can feel infantilising and is emotionally complex. But support is one of the most powerful forms of treatment; without it, medication compliance is low and relapse is high.
11regulation
So we essentially at this point have two forms of treatment that are going on, medication, which can change state via your body chemistry, and then essentially the other people around you, who can be supports, sources of happiness and grounding, this could be therapists and so on. The next part of a wellness plan is to try and manage all of the things that can alter your state, in such a way that you don't go too low, and don't go too high, that your state is not transitioning too rapidly or acutely. I spent far too many tokens on this diagram, but what I am trying to show is that there are things all around that dysregulate, I've put them in red, and you want to try and counterbalance them with things that regulate, which I've put in green. In a sense, regulation is the most active form of treatment, it's taking specific actions, making changes, or whatever, to manage your state yourself. The very fact that it is active means that it is effort, and this in itself can be challenge, at both ends of the spectrum for example medication compliance can drop - in a prolonged depressive state it is understandable to want to feel happier. In a more impulsive state or higher state, your own symptoms you might not recognise.
12regulators-dysregulators
Sleep: Probably the most important. Protected, regular, long vs late nights, jet lag, stress. Rhythm: We are very embodied creatures. Our psychological states are intensely related to our physical states. Regularity is essential, routine seems to make people happier. Stable - food, exercise, social contact (IPSRT) vs chaotic, intense then resting, circadian. We are very organised embodied beings. Stress: Manageable, downshifts, resets, interventions, multitasking and context switching (is this focus?) vs chaos, conflict, shifting. Substances: SUD disorder high, but bipolar always treated pharmalogically. Awareness: Simply learning about your state, about your condition, how things can get bad but can also recover, what works for you, what doesn't. People: People who know, accountable, empowered vs isolation, hiding. This will be different for different people - for many people family is regulating and supporting, but for some people it is not. Some friends may support in different ways. Therapy and Integration: Integration of experience is important. Lots of input, lots of anxiety, changes to lifestyle, as well as talking about these to other people you yourself need to find ways to integrate these experiences, to try and be at peace with them and where you are, to be forgiving. Other surprising things: Goal attainment, addiction, changes to foundational beliefs, anti-depressants, excitement, family, sunny days, good news, intense exercise, pregnancy and postpartum, pets (anchor). All in all, if you are lucky and have agency there is much you can control here. Some of these changes might be small, but depending on people's circumstances some of these changes will be very difficult to make - again that's where therapy and support comes in, as well as potentially more medication support during adjustment.
13pathologising-happiness
When you know that tweaking up here is high risk, you want to avoid it. Particularly because at a certain point you might not even know where you are, and at that point all bets are off. So; you start to pathologise everything. Wow, I'm actually feeling happy. Shit, am I feeling too happy? Wow, I've actually been sleeping well, am I sleeping too much? Wow, I'm getting really really into this, I just lost myself for hours, that was so much fun, is that OK? This continuous self monitoring itself is anxiety inducing, and also depressing - what is given attention is awkward. Am I starting to shut off from the outside world or live in my own? Am I disconnecting? Have my foundational believes broken down? This is hyper-awareness and pathologisation of everything behaviour and mood related. So I have spent and spent a long time pathologising all sorts of behaviours, thoughts, whatever, with a particular focus on what might be manic, hyper-creative, overly stimulating, disconnecting, whatever, and here we are now in the world of AI.

15ai-core-beliefs
An initially surprising dysregulator is a challenge to core beliefs. I learned this from a psychotherapist who went to a five-day meditation retreat and shortly afterwards had a severe psychotic episode; she believes the cause was a challenge to her core beliefs. One of my first challenges with AI was exactly this. I couldn't get it out of my head - my craft had changed overnight, I couldn't believe the output, everything felt like it was changing. The way I approach every problem had changed, my future plans were invalidated, my sense of self-worth was invalidated. It is an emotional, even grief-ridden journey, and I think many of us are on it, challenged by it again and again.
16ai-integration
These experiences have to be integrated. We need time to process complex stuff. Some processing is done in the foreground, some is done in the background, and when the backlog of things to process gets too long, we tend to struggle. So much of this technology is changing things in fundamental ways, it is not surprising that so many people I speak to are excited - somewhat - but also exhausted. Integration is effort, it is work, it needs the right environment and space. Actively integrating experience. How do I feel about this. What does this mean. Are these changes making me feel more secure, or less secure?
17ai-addiction
Mania is broadly hyperdopaminergic with reward hypersensitivity - the same wiring that makes the bipolar brain prone to mood elevation makes it prone to addictive loop. SUD between 40-60%. Long term effects are extremely harmful. Any short cycle dopamine loop is can be addictive. All unmanaged or uncontrolled addiction is maladaptive and harmful. How addiction is framed is largely a socialogical effect. Alcohol addiction is perceived to be maladaptive. Workaolism is perceived in a more complex way. But if you look at the questions that are asked when talking about addiction - i.e. If you are finding yourself unable to stop, itching for more, if AI is affecting your social connections, your work, your focus, then you are managing addiction. Managing addiction is incredibly hard. Therapy and reflection helps you understand and recognise addiction; the rates of SUD, AUD and other types of addiction are incredibly high in bipolar disorder, comorbitity is very high, and the effects are some of the most serious, particularly in the long term. You become vigilant.
18ai-managing-addiction
Be aware that there is a loop. Balance it out with regulators. Create space from sources or triggers, create friction if needed. Commit to small ways to manage over time. Prepare for relapse and show compassion.

23ai-attention
You can do so much. You can pretty much do anything, you can make machines to do anything. You can make machines to make machines to do anything. We are all inveterate tool builders and problem solvers, and now we have incredibly powerful tools. Engineers tend to create their own universes. So we can do almost anything, or at least try, so we start doing everything. Multi-threading, every time we kick off a long horizon task and have to wait for a few seconds, we kick off another one. We over-parallelising, racing thoughts, jittery, distracted. All symptoms of hypomania or mania, all great big red flags, all addiction and dopemine loop related, cause it is fun but also tiring. This was the biggest message I got when I asked around from people what they were finding hard about this world - a catastrophic drop in the ability to focus, tiredness, and also depression and fatigue.
24ai-focus
Just be aware. There is tonnes of stuff out there. But largely you are looking for mindfulness, being present, and then starting to build habits. Some examples: 1. Rule of three: don't build a tool till the third time. 2. Single thread short tasks and run them to completion. 3. Offload long tasks, but guard when they can interrupt. 4. Create protected time, know your rhythm, your rest time. 5. Prioritise.
25ai-pressured-speaking
Pressured speaking is affect from states like hypomania, essentially talking rapidly, in one-directional way. None stop, extremely hard to interrupt, a wall of thought or stream of consciousness. AI is very good at pressured speaking. You ask a question and get an essay, can't keep up with what is coming out, more than you can process. Rapid, hard to interrupt, one directional. So pressured speaking is a pain to listen to. I'm sure most of us have smashed the escape key or ctrlc a bunch of times kind of thinging ughhh please shut up just give me the quick answer. So it is tiring when someone is speaking at you, but what is even worse, is when your human buddies start sending over all the unfiltered, unedited, stream of consciousness from _their_ AI.
