9treatment-regulation
There are many elements of 'wellness management'. Treatment means pharmacotherapy and psychotherapy. Medication is a foundation, psychotherapy reduces relapse, non-compliance, and develops the skills to regulate. Medication typically means mood stabalisers, anti-psychotics, anti-depressants, anxiolytics, sleep medication, and so on. Mood stabalisers are used to try and anchor state and will generally be core to treatment. There a few different types, such as lithium, and people react very differently to them. Titration onto these medications is generally a slow process, as side effects have to be monitored and people react differently. Certain types of anti-depressants might be used to soften the lower states, these also have to be carefully monitored, and in fact many well-known anti-depressants used for treating unipolar depression are known to worsen symptoms of bipolar disorder. For the states at the higher end of the spectrum, sedatives can be used, hypnotics for sleep, and anti-psychotics. A medical regime often takes a long time to establish, and needs monitoring and adjustment, and there will be compromises - some medications will reduce the risk of mania, but increase sedation for example. Anti-depressants may need to be balanced with anti-psychotics, and these are all state or mind altering drugs with considerable side effects. However, over time many people will become good at monitoring their state and be able to adjust medication. *Clock: 7:06*

29ai-ipsrt
So why have I talked about all of this? Because it has made me happier to recognise it all. I have had to change my lifestyle, considerably. Some of these changes have been hard, but largely most have made me happier and more secure. And in fact the awareness that comes from these changes has made me more well equiped for other scenarios in life. IPSRT (Interpersonal and Social Rhythm Therapy, Ellen Frank) - structured therapy for bipolar disorder. Two threads: stable daily rhythm (sleep, meals, activity, social contact) to stabilise mood, and addressing interpersonal events that disrupt rhythm (grief, role changes, conflict). Evidence-based, reduces relapse, one of the few therapies with a case as monotherapy for bipolar II depression. Same principle applied to engineering: protected hours, predictable cadence (reviews, releases, standups), real time with real people, and repair when AI-driven sprints have strained collaboration. Almost every dysregulator we named - fragmented attention, pressured output, over-parallelisation, grandiose feedback loops, world-building that outruns the model - comes from rhythm collapsing under AI-native pace. Rhythm is the cheapest, oldest regulator we have. AI makes it the easiest to lose. *Clock: 29:10*
